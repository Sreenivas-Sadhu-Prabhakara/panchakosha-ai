🏛️ Level 3 Capstone

Explain an LLM to a smart friend

The surest test of understanding is teaching. Write (or record) a short, correct explainer that takes a smart non-expert from "text goes in" all the way to "why it sometimes makes things up" — in plain language, with one analogy and one simple diagram.

The goal

Produce a clear, jargon-light explainer of about 500 words (or a 3-minute talk) that walks in order through tokens → the transformer & attention → training and RLHF → why it hallucinates. Use exactly one through-line analogy and one simple diagram. If a curious friend gets it, you've got it.

The steps

① Draft the spine

Write the path in order, one sentence per step first: tokens → embeddings → transformer/attention → training (pretrain → fine-tune → RLHF) → why it hallucinates. Get the sequence right before you flesh it out.

starter
I'm writing a ~500-word explainer of how an LLM works, for a smart friend with no ML background. Here is my one-sentence-per-step outline: [paste]. Point out anything inaccurate or out of order before I expand it.

② Find one analogy

Pick a single through-line analogy (a super-well-read autocomplete, a fluent improviser who never checks a fact, etc.) and use it consistently. One good analogy beats five half-ones.

starter
Give me three candidate analogies for "next-token prediction" aimed at a non-expert. For each, note where the analogy breaks down, so I don't accidentally mislead my reader.

③ Draw one simple diagram

Sketch a text/ASCII pipeline the reader can grasp at a glance: text → tokens → embeddings → transformer → next token. Keep it to one look; resist adding detail.

starter
Here is my ASCII diagram of the LLM pipeline: [paste]. Is the order correct and are the labels accurate? Suggest a simpler version if mine is cluttered.

④ Explain the failure mode

In plain words, say why the model can be fluent and wrong — that it predicts plausible text rather than looking facts up — and what the reader should do about it (verify anything that matters).

starter
Read my paragraph on hallucination. Is it accurate to say the model predicts plausible text rather than looking facts up? Flag any overstatement or anything technically wrong: [paste]

⑤ Critique it for accuracy

Have the model fact-check your explainer and flag any claim that's false, oversimplified to the point of being wrong, or hype. Fix what it catches — you stay the author.

starter
Critique this explainer for technical accuracy for a general audience. List anything false, oversimplified into being wrong, or hyped. Don't rewrite it — just list the issues so I can fix them myself: [paste]

⑥ Test it on a human

Read it to someone non-technical (or ask the model to role-play one). If they can hand you back a rough version of token, prediction, and why it can be wrong, it works.

starter
Pretend you're a curious person with no tech background. Read my explainer, then tell me in your own words: what a token is, what the model is really doing, and why it can be confidently wrong: [paste]

What to hand in

One piece — a document, a page, or a 3-minute recording — titled "How an LLM works (for a smart friend)", containing your ~500-word explainer, the one analogy you chose, and the one diagram. Optionally include the model's accuracy critique and a line on what you changed because of it.

◆ Success criteria — you've passed when…

✓ It correctly covers tokens, next-token prediction, training (including RLHF), and why it hallucinates.
✓ A non-expert can read it once and re-explain the gist back to you.
✓ It uses exactly one clear analogy and one simple diagram.
✓ No claim in it is actually false — you checked the hard ones against a source.

▸ Stretch goal

Record it as a 3-minute video or voice note — teaching out loud exposes the gaps fast. Or add one final paragraph on RAG: how feeding the model real source text patches the "makes things up" problem you just described.
