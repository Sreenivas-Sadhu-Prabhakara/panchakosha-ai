Lesson 1.0

The landscape: what these tools are

An AI assistant (also called a chatbot or large language model) is a program you talk to in plain language. You type or speak a request; it writes back. Unlike a search engine, it doesn't just find pages — it composes an answer for you: a draft email, a summary, a plan, an explanation, an image.

You don't need to pick the "one right" tool. Here's the honest 2026 lay of the land — all have capable free tiers:

  • Claude (Anthropic) — thoughtful writing, analysis, and working with long documents. Used for the code examples later in this path.
  • ChatGPT (OpenAI) — the household name; strong all-rounder with images and voice.
  • Gemini (Google) — deeply tied into Search, Gmail, and Docs.
  • Microsoft Copilot — built into Windows and Office.
  • Perplexity — answers with live web sources cited, great for research.
◆ Insight

They all work the same way underneath (you'll see exactly how in Level 3). For now: pick one, open it in a browser, and keep it in a pinned tab. Familiarity beats choosing perfectly.

Lesson 1.1

Your first conversation

Talking to an assistant is a conversation, not a single command. Your first message rarely lands perfectly — the skill is in the follow-up. Ask, read, then refine: "make it shorter," "more formal," "add a line about the delay."

Try this exact prompt in any assistant to feel the loop:

prompt
Help me reply to this text from a friend. Keep it warm, one short paragraph, and suggest Saturday instead:

"Hey! Are we still on for lunch Friday?"

Now follow up with "make it a little funnier" and watch it revise. That back-and-forth is the core skill of Level 1.

▸ Try it

Send the prompt above, then send three follow-ups in a row: shorter, warmer, and "add an emoji." Notice you never had to re-explain the context — the assistant remembers the conversation.

Lesson 1.2

Prompting basics: the C-E-F-T framework

A good prompt usually has four parts. You won't always need all four, but reaching for them turns a vague reply into a useful one:

  • Context — who you are and the situation. "I'm a nurse writing to a patient's family."
  • Example — show, don't just tell. Paste a sample you like the tone of.
  • Format — how you want the answer. "Give me 3 bullet points."
  • Tone — the voice. "Warm but professional."

Compare a weak prompt to a C-E-F-T one:

weak
write a thank you email
C·E·F·T
Context: I'm a freelance photographer. A client just paid on time for a wedding shoot.
Format: a short email, 4 sentences max, with a subject line.
Tone: genuinely warm, not salesy.
Task: write a thank-you note and gently mention I offer 20% off prints this month.
● Note

Don't memorize the letters — just ask yourself "did I say who, show an example, name the format, and set the tone?" The reference pack has this as a one-page cheat sheet.

Lesson 1.3

Everyday wins

Here's where it becomes part of daily life. Copy any of these and swap in your own details:

planning
Plan a simple 5-day dinner menu for 2 people. We're vegetarian, don't like mushrooms, and want each meal under 30 minutes. Give me a shopping list grouped by aisle.
explain
Explain how a mortgage APR is different from the interest rate, like I'm 15. Use one real number example.
summarize
Here's a long email thread. Summarize what was decided, who owns what, and any open question, in 5 bullets:

[paste the thread]
◆ Insight

The pattern behind every win: bring your real material (your thread, your numbers, your constraints) and ask for a specific shape of answer. Generic questions get generic answers.

Lesson 1.4

Working with documents & images

Most assistants let you attach a file or photo (look for a paperclip or "+" near the message box). This unlocks a lot:

  • Upload a PDF (a lease, a report, a manual) and ask questions about it.
  • Photograph a form, receipt, or label and ask it to read or explain it.
  • Snap a fridge shelf and ask for recipes from what it sees.
  • Ask it to generate an image — a party invite, a logo sketch, a diagram.
with a PDF attached
I've attached my apartment lease. In plain English: what's my notice period to move out, and are there any fees if I leave early? Quote the exact clause.
▲ Careful

The assistant can misread a document. For anything that matters — money, health, legal — treat its answer as a fast first read, then verify the quoted clause yourself. You'll learn why it can be wrong in Level 3.

Lesson 1.5

Research & fact-checking

Assistants are brilliant at explaining and drafting — but they can state wrong things confidently. This is called a hallucination, and learning to catch it is the single most important habit in this whole course.

Three defenses:

  1. Ask for sources. "Give me links I can check." Tools like Perplexity, or Claude/ChatGPT/Gemini with web search on, cite as they go.
  2. Ask it to show its uncertainty. Add: "If you're not sure, say so."
  3. Verify anything consequential against a primary source — the official site, the actual document.
research, safely
What are the current carry-on liquid limits for flights within the EU? Give me the rule, the source link, and flag if this may have changed recently or varies by airport.
● Note

Good rule of thumb: use AI to understand and draft freely; use AI to state facts only with a source you can click.

Lesson 1.6

Voice, mobile & assistants on the go

The phone apps for Claude, ChatGPT, Gemini and Copilot all support voice — tap the mic and just talk. It's perfect for hands-free moments: cooking, walking, driving (safely).

  • Voice mode — have a spoken back-and-forth, like a phone call.
  • Camera — point at something and ask about it in real time.
  • Share sheet — highlight text in any app and "share to" your assistant to summarize or reply.
▸ Try it

Open your assistant's phone app and say out loud: "I have chicken, rice, and a lime. Talk me through a dinner while I cook." Then just follow along, hands-free.

Lesson 1.7

Safety, privacy & etiquette

A short, important lesson. Treat an AI chat like a helpful stranger who takes notes:

  • Never paste secrets you wouldn't want stored: passwords, full card numbers, government IDs, someone else's private data.
  • Check the memory/training setting. Most tools let you turn off using your chats for training, and offer a "temporary chat" mode.
  • You're accountable for the output. If you send it, it's yours — read before you hit send.
  • Be fair. Don't use it to impersonate, deceive, or plagiarize. Disclose AI help where honesty calls for it.
▲ Careful

The golden test before pasting anything: "Would I be comfortable if this appeared in a data breach?" If not, redact it first.
